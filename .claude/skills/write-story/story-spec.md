# Christopher & Benjamine's Storybook — Complete Master Spec

---

## THE FAMILY

- **Christopher:** Big brother (~5). Strong, brave, loves to explore. Carries the weight of being "the brave one" — but sometimes he's scared too and can't tell anyone. He protects Benjamine. At age 5, having a younger sibling is making him emotionally smarter than any experience could — but he is practising emotional intelligence *outward*, toward Benjamine, before he has learned to turn it inward. His growth arc is not becoming braver. It is learning to apply to himself the care he gives to his brother.
- **Benjamine:** Little brother (~3). Curious, silly, follows Christopher everywhere. Smaller voice but often the bravest when it counts. Growing from clingy to independent across stories. Says things bluntly and honestly. At age 3, he is linguistically and emotionally more advanced than his age because he has Christopher as a model. His bluntness isn't naivety — it's developmental honesty before social filtering arrives. His bravery doesn't come from deciding. It comes from not stopping to think about it.
- **Together:** The bedtime stories are the emotional mirror these two boys use to replay difficult experiences safely. They give real feelings a universe to live in for eight pages. In that universe, the feelings survive.
- **Mommy and Daddy:** Referenced lovingly. They "always come" later to sleep in the same bed. They are safety anchors but NOT active characters in most stories — their presence is felt, not shown. They are ALWAYS safe, ALWAYS coming.
- The brothers share a bed. This is central to the stories — togetherness at bedtime is the emotional core.
- **The family lives in Australia.** This is the home base — the place they leave from and return to.

## THIS IS A TEXT-ONLY APP

There are NO illustrations. A parent reads these stories aloud in the dark or dim light. Every image must be created with WORDS ALONE. This changes everything about how you write:

- **Words are the illustrations.** Sound, smell, touch, taste, and temperature do the work that pictures do in a book.
- **Write for the ear, not the eye.** (Margaret Wise Brown: "Children read with their senses, whereas adults read with their eyes.")
- **Write for the mouth.** Every sentence must feel good to say aloud. If a parent stumbles reading it, rewrite it.
- **Breath-length sentences.** Each line should be comfortably spoken in one breath.
- **Sensory description is your primary illustration tool.** Describe what things sound like, feel like, smell like — let the words paint the picture without sound effects or capitalised emphasis.
- **The pause is your page turn.** Use "..." and short sentences to create beats where the parent naturally pauses. The silence IS the illustration.

### The Mouth Test (Mem Fox Method)
Before any story is final:
1. Read the entire thing aloud, start to finish
2. Mark every place you stumble, pause unnaturally, or lose breath
3. Rewrite those passages
4. Read aloud again from the top
5. Repeat until zero stumbles
6. Test: Can a 3-year-old join in on the repeating parts by the third iteration?

## AGE TARGETING

Every story must specify an ageRange.

### Ages 2-3 (Benjamine's Range — Short & Simple)
- 3-4 pages, 100-200 words total, 2 min read
- Sentences: 4-6 words average. One idea per sentence.
- Vocabulary: only words a 2-year-old uses daily
- Emotion: ONE feeling per story. Don't layer fears.
- Structure: simple repetition pattern (goodnight toes, goodnight knees...)
- Ending: short, direct. "Warm. Soft. Goodnight."
- Benjamine is the main character. Christopher is the anchor.
- Best categories: bedtime, feelings

### Ages 3-5 (Both Brothers — Standard)
- 5-7 pages, 300-600 words total, 3-5 min read
- Most stories live here. Full emotional arc, humor.
- Both brothers active. Dialogue-driven.
- All categories work.

### Ages 4-5 (Christopher Leads — Longer & Deeper)
- 7-9 pages, 600-900 words MAX, 5 min read
- Complex emotions: being the big one, leadership, separation
- Christopher is the emotional lead. Benjamine is the grounding force.
- Can explore what it means to be brave, why grown-ups get scared too
- **WARNING:** May be too long for school-night bedtime. Mark "weekend reads."

## STORY STRUCTURES

Use these proven structures from the master authors. Pick the one that fits the story.

### 1. Home-Away-Home (Circular) — *Sendak, Jeffers*
The story starts and ends in the same place (their bed), but the boys have changed inside. This is the PRIMARY structure for travel/adventure stories.
1. **Home/Safety** — Boys in bed, the familiar
2. **Departure** — Something pulls them out (curiosity, a sound, an invitation)
3. **Adventure** — Series of encounters in the new world
4. **Transformation** — They learn or feel something new
5. **Return Home** — Back to bed, but they see it differently
- *"And his supper was still warm"* — love waits unconditionally at the end.

### 2. Three Encounters — *Donaldson, folk tales*
The protagonist meets exactly three obstacles/characters following an identical pattern with small variations. The third encounter either breaks the pattern or resolves it.
1. Encounter 1 — establish the pattern
2. Encounter 2 — confirm the pattern (child joins in)
3. Encounter 3 — break or complete the pattern (surprise/resolution)

### 3. Call-and-Response Journey — *Rosen*
A sequence of obstacles with identical structure:
1. Announce the obstacle
2. Declare you can't avoid it ("We can't go over it. We can't go under it.")
3. Commit to facing it ("We've got to go through it!")
4. Onomatopoeic immersion (unique sound for each obstacle)
- The return journey compresses into a single rush — dramatic acceleration.

### 4. Accumulation Ladder — *Carle, Dodd*
Each page adds one more element while repeating all previous ones. The snowball effect. Energy builds until a single event disrupts everything.

### 5. Catalogue/Ritual — *Margaret Wise Brown*
No plot. Systematic naming of familiar objects with hypnotic repetition. Move from concrete to abstract. The rhythm IS the story. **Best for pure bedtime wind-down.**

### 6. Escalating Absurdity — *Kerr*
One impossible thing placed in a completely ordinary setting. Never explain why. Let the absurdity grow. End with domestic comfort restored.

## THE REPETITION ENGINE

Repetition is the PRIMARY engagement mechanism for ages 2-5. Not decoration — structure.

### For every repeating section, define:
- **The Frame:** The sentence structure that stays identical
- **The Variable:** The single element that changes each time
- **The Sound:** An onomatopoeic word unique to each iteration
- **The Escalation:** How each repetition slightly raises the stakes or energy

### The Four Types of Repetition:

1. **The Refrain** — A recurring line at predictable intervals, like a song chorus. Children join in. ("But the bear snores on.")
2. **Cumulative** — Each repetition adds a new element, repeating all previous ones. The snowball.
3. **Call-and-Response** — Interactive back-and-forth that invites the listener to participate.
4. **Pattern Interruption** — Establish a pattern, then deliberately break it for surprise or emotion.

### The Critical Rule:
Each repetition must advance the story or deepen understanding. Ask: "What is different this time?" If nothing — cut it.

### Break the Pattern Exactly Once:
Carle's Saturday binge. Rosen's reversed return. Dodd's scattering event. The single disruption creates the climactic surprise BECAUSE the pattern was so reliable.

---

## THE DUAL-AGE SOLUTION

Write the repeating frame at **Benjamine's level** (he joins in on the chorus). Write the narrative connective tissue at **Christopher's level** (he follows the plot). This is exactly what Rosen does — the chorus is age-2 accessible while the framing is age-5 accessible.

## WRITING FOR THE EAR — STYLE RULES

### Sentence Rhythm
- Short fragments during action/tension: "Gone." / "Out." / "Nope."
- Stack fragments for momentum: "Knees. Tummy. Touched the tile."
- Long flowing sentences ONLY during calm/magical moments
- Vary sentence length deliberately — this IS your pacing. Short = energy. Long = calm.

### Sensory Language (No Sound Effects or ALL CAPS)
- **No onomatopoeia or ALL CAPS sound effects.** No SPLASH, VROOM, WHOOSH, etc. Describe what things sound like, feel like, smell like using normal words.
- **No capitalised words for emphasis.** Not "SO warm" — just "so warm." Let the sentence structure carry the emphasis.
- **Describe sounds naturally:** "The rain tapped on the roof." Not "PATTER PATTER PATTER."
- **Each location gets a sensory signature through description.** Malaysia's night market: the smell of smoke and spice, the sizzle of meat on coals. Singapore: warm rain on skin, lights reflected on still water.

### Dialogue Rules
- 1-2 lines per character MAX before a reaction
- Rapid-fire ping-pong for high energy: "Ready?" "No! But go anyway!"
- Kids say things bluntly: "You're not very nice."
- "..." before vulnerable admissions
- Trailing ellipsis for falling asleep

### Word Choice
- Words a 2-5 year old knows. If a grown-up would explain it, don't use it.
- Bad words: "thorough," "enormous," "anxious" — use "SO big," "scared," "tummy"
- Physical comparisons: "soft as pillows," "no bigger than a tennis ball"
- **Powerful verbs over adjectives:** "He crept" not "He walked quietly"
- Dr. Seuss principle: **Severe constraints produce sharper writing.** Limit vocabulary ruthlessly.

### Sensory Stacking (Your Illustrations)
Every scene needs 2-3 senses (not all five — overwhelm kills immersion):

| Sense | Technique | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Sound** | Onomatopoeia, rhythm | "The rain went *plink-plink-plink* on the roof" |
| **Touch** | Texture, temperature | "The sand was warm and scratchy between his toes" |
| **Smell** | Specific, evocative | "The air smelled like wet leaves and somebody's dinner" |
| **Taste** | Concrete, familiar | "Salty like the ocean. Sweet like mango ice" |
| **Feel** | Body sensations | "The warm sticky air sat on his skin like a blanket" |

**Be specific, not generic:** "Smelled like warm bread" not "smelled good."
**Use sensory details children have actually experienced:** Rain on skin, sand in toes, the smell of bath time.

### Direct Address
Pull the child into the story. "And do you know what happened next?" This is an oral tradition technique. Use sparingly but powerfully.

### The Pause Technique
Leave beats for the parent to pause. "He looked down. And there... right there..." The silence IS the illustration.

## EMOTION RULES

### The Sendak Principle
Never lie about feelings. Children know when you're sanitizing. Show the real emotion (anger, fear, loneliness) and show it being MASTERED — not eliminated. Love waits unconditionally at the end.

### Core Rules
- NEVER dismiss fear. Not even once. Not as a joke.
- Bravery = scared AND doing it anyway. Never "not being scared."
- Every progress celebrated: "Just my FEET!" -> "FEET COUNT!"
- "Permission to stop" moment — it's okay to not be ready today
- The kids must be the ones who ACT — grown-ups never solve it FOR them
- Helping someone else be brave makes you forget your own fear

### The "Me Too" Moment
Every story MUST have one moment where someone admits they feel the same way, with **specific physical detail**:
- Not: "I get scared too."
- Instead: "Christopher sat very still. His fingers squeezed the edge of the blanket. 'My tummy goes funny too,' he said quietly."

### The Heat Before the Cooling
Every fear must be felt for a minimum of **one full page** before any character begins to help, joke, or comfort. A fear named and immediately soothed teaches nothing. A fear that is held allows the child to feel it, confirm it's survivable, and receive the comfort as something earned.

### The Slow Moment Rule
Every story must have one moment where something difficult sits for **at least two sentences** before anyone responds, fixes, or makes it funny.

Not: "Christopher felt scared. But then Benjamine squeezed his hand."
Instead: "Christopher felt scared. He didn't say anything. His hands stayed very still. Benjamine looked at him."

### The Goldilocks Effect
Children's attention is optimized by "moderate surprise" — events that violate expectations enough to be interesting but not so much that they're confusing. 80% familiar, 20% surprising.

## TRAVEL & ADVENTURE STORIES

The family lives in Australia. The boys travel to real places around the world. These are **sensory tourism** stories, not geography lessons.

### The Rules of Sensory Tourism
1. **Lead with the body, not the map.** Don't describe where a place is. Describe what it FEELS like to be there.
2. **One location = one sensory signature.** Each place has a distinct feel:
   - Malaysia = warm sticky air, the SIZZLE of satay, sweet roti, the call of hornbills
   - Singapore = lights on water, the rush of the MRT, laksa steam, Supertrees glowing
   - Japan = wind chimes (ting-ting-ting), cold noodles slurped, cherry blossom petals on skin
   - Kenya = red dust on toes, the rumble of elephants, stars SO close you could touch them
   - Iceland = cold that bites your nose, WHOOOOSH of geysers, puffins with clown faces
3. **Embed culture in action, not explanation.** The boys don't "learn about" a place. They DO things there. They eat. They run. They splash.
   - Not: "In Malaysia, people eat with their hands."
   - Instead: "Christopher looked at his plate. No fork. No spoon. Daddy was scooping rice with his fingers. Christopher tried it. The rice was warm and sticky and perfect."
4. **Mirror the child's perspective.** Young children in a new place notice: weird foods, funny sounds, different animals, how the rain is different. Write from THAT perspective.
5. **Never explain what you can show.** Sensory-first, geography-never.
6. **The familiar emotional through-line.** The story is about something universal (being brave, trying new things, missing home, making a friend) that happens to take place in a specific location.

### Travel Story Structure (Home-Away-Home)
1. **Bed in Australia** — Cozy, safe, familiar
2. **The Journey** — How they get there (plane, dream, magic — keep it simple)
3. **Arrival** — Hit them with the sensory signature immediately. First thing they FEEL.
4. **Three Encounters** — Rule of Three. Three things they see/do/taste/try in the new place.
5. **The Moment** — Something brave, something kind, something that changes them
6. **Return to Bed** — Back home, but they bring something back (a feeling, a word, a memory)
7. **Sleep** — Warm, safe, together. The adventure glows inside them.

### Cultural Respect
- Show diversity WITHIN cultures, not monolithic portrayals
- Depict contemporary life, not stereotypes
- Be specific about place — "Malaysia" not "Asia"
- The foreign setting should feel as normal to its people as Australia feels to the boys
- Don't frame difference as spectacle — frame it as discovery

## HUMANISING THE STORY

### The Observed Moment Requirement
Before writing any story, complete: **"Something I noticed Christopher or Benjamine do this week:"**

Must be a specific physical action — not a feeling, not a theme. It must appear in the story exactly once, quietly, without comment.

Examples:
- "Christopher always gets very still when he's deciding something. Not quiet. Still."
- "Benjamine pronounces a word wrong and refuses to be corrected."
- "When Christopher is unsure, he looks at the floor and to the left."

### The Purposeless True Detail
Every story must contain one detail about Christopher or Benjamine that has **no narrative function**. Not therapeutic. Not plot. Simply something true about one of the boys.

Must come early (first two pages). Must be specific to THEM — if it could appear in a story about any child, it's wrong.

Bad: "Christopher felt scared."
Human: "Christopher grabbed the corner of the pillowcase with two fingers. He always did that."

### The Accident Rule
Every story is allowed one moment that breaks the system slightly. A page that runs long. A joke with no purpose. A character saying something that goes nowhere but feels right.

If it surprised you while writing, keep it. Mistakes feel wrong on re-read. Discoveries feel more right the second time.

### The Discovery Question
After writing, ask: **"Did I find out anything while writing this?"**

If the story went exactly where expected — nothing surprised you — go back and find one moment of uncertainty. Write into it.

## STORY STRUCTURE

### Format
- Pages: 5-7 per story (3-4 for ages 2-3)
- ~80-120 words per page
- Use \n for paragraph breaks within a page

### Stage Directions for the Parent
Every story includes **inline stage directions** telling the parent how to read — when to pause, whisper, slow down, speed up, etc. These are rendered in the app as small italic text in a lighter colour, visually distinct from story text.

**Format:** Directions go in parentheses on their own line BEFORE the text they affect, or inline before a specific sentence. The parent must see the direction BEFORE they read the line.

**The Direction Vocabulary (use only these):**

| Direction | Meaning |
|---|---|
| *(softly)* | Lower your voice, gentle |
| *(whisper)* | Barely audible |
| *(louder)* | Raise energy, not yelling |
| *(slowly)* | Stretch the words out |
| *(pause — N seconds)* | Stop talking for N seconds. Always specify the number. |
| *(long pause — N seconds)* | A longer silence. The silence IS the story. Always specify. |
| *(excited)* | Fast, bright, higher pitch |
| *(matter-of-fact)* | Flat, normal, no drama |
| *(trailing off)* | Voice fades, slows to nothing |
| *(warmly)* | Smile in your voice |

**Rules:**
- 2-4 directions per page. Never more. Over-directing is worse than under-directing.
- Use directions at **transitions** — when energy, volume, or pace CHANGES. If the next line is the same energy as the last, no direction needed.
- Pauses must always include the number of seconds: *(pause — 3 seconds)* not just *(pause)*.
- The final page should use *(whisper)*, *(trailing off)*, and longer pauses to wind down.
- Directions can be combined: *(slowly, almost a whisper)* or *(excited, a little faster)*.

### Page Turn Power
Each page should end with a reason to keep going:
- **Question Turn:** End with a question. Next page reveals the answer.
- **Action Turn:** Character is about to act. Next page shows result.
- **Ellipsis Turn:** "And behind the door was..."
- **Pattern-Break Turn:** The established pattern finally changes.
Not every turn needs to be dramatic — vary between reveals and quieter progressions.

### Opening (by category)
- **Bedtime:** Start quiet. Something gentle and unexpected.
- **Adventure/Travel:** Start in bed. Then — a sound, a feeling, a pull toward somewhere new.
- **Feelings:** Start with the FEELING. Name it in the body first.

### Middle — The Architecture
1. **The real feeling** — a fear, wonder, or resistance kids actually experience
2. **The "me too" moment** — someone admits they feel it too
3. **The action** — the boys DO something (tiny-brave counts)
4. **The discovery** — they find something unexpected about the place or themselves

### Ending — The Bedtime Deceleration
- Second-to-last page: emotional resolution (the hug, the realization, the return)
- LAST PAGE: wind-down
  - Physical calming, sensory warmth
  - Collapse distance between story world and child's real bed
  - Sleep language: *safe, cozy, warm, snuggled, dream, stars, moon, quiet, still, home*
- LAST LINE must be:
  - An image or feeling — NOT dialogue
  - Short fragments or one clean sentence
  - Warmth, safety, togetherness
- **NO all-caps sounds in final 2 pages.** The story whispers itself to sleep.

### The Bedtime Deceleration Curve
```
Energy:  Low ——> Rise ——> Moderate Peak ——> Descend ——> Lowest
         Cozy    Curiosity   Adventure       Return     Sleep
```

## CHARACTER ARCS

### Benjamine's Arc
- Early stories: Clingy, scared, follows Christopher. Whispers. Lip wobbles.
- Middle stories: Starting to act independently. "I'll go with you."
- Later stories: Brave in his own quiet way. Direct, physical, honest. Doesn't overthink it.
- NEVER regress without reason.

### Christopher's Arc
- Early stories: The protector. Brave for Benjamine's sake. Hides his own fear.
- Middle stories: Starting to name feelings. "My brain won't stop talking."
- Later stories: Can be vulnerable AND strong. Doesn't have to choose.
- Growth = learning he doesn't have to be brave ALONE.

## CREATING NEW CHARACTERS

When the boys travel, they may meet people and creatures. Keep it simple:

- Max ONE new character per story
- THREE things on first appearance:
  1. Visual quirk you can describe in one sentence
  2. Personality in one line
  3. Surprise vulnerability that comes out LATER
- Give them a verbal tic or catchphrase
- Characters who appear once should feel like they COULD come back

---

## WHAT NOT TO DO

- Don't lecture (3+ lines of explanation = break it up)
- Don't summarize the moral ("And they learned that...")
- Don't use baby talk
- Don't put Mommy and Daddy in danger or make them absent
- Don't leave scary things unresolved
- Don't have genuinely threatening villains
- Don't let stories run long
- Don't describe what you should make them HEAR, SMELL, or FEEL
- Don't teach geography — create sensory experiences
- Don't write action for bedtime endings
- Don't explain a culture — let the boys LIVE in it for a page

## FRAMEWORKS FROM THE MASTERS

### The Margaret Wise Brown Test
"Does it jog them with the unexpected and comfort them with the familiar?" Every story needs BOTH surprise (20%) and safety (80%).

### The Mem Fox Standard
Maximum 600 words for standard stories. "The biggest fault of wannabe picture-book writers is to write too much." Expect 40+ revisions. Rhythm is non-negotiable.

### The Michael Rosen Method
Build a rigid call-and-response structure. Give each scene its own sound-word. The predictability IS the engagement — children join in physically.

### The Sendak Truth
"Too many parents and too many writers of children's books don't respect the fact that kids know a great deal and suffer a great deal." Never sanitize. Show real feelings being mastered.

### The Judith Kerr Test
"You watch your child's face and leave out bits if they look bored." The child's reaction is the only valid test.

### The Dr. Seuss Constraint
Severe vocabulary limits produce sharper writing. If you can say it simpler, you must.

### The Oliver Jeffers Economy
"When you get down to using as few words as I do, they all count. It's probably more like writing poetry than prose."

## FINAL QUALITY CHECKS

### The 3-Year-Old Test
Read at bedtime pace. Is Benjamine still listening on page 4? If not — too long, too abstract, too slow. Cut.

### The Mouth Test
Can a parent read every sentence without stumbling? Do the words feel good in the mouth? Is every sentence one comfortable breath?

### The Body Test
Does every important moment happen in a body first?
- Fear = squeezy, still, tummy tight
- Bravery = a foot moving, a hand reaching
- Comfort = warmth, breathing slowing, something touching something

### The Sound Test
Close your eyes. Can you HEAR the story? Do the onomatopoeia paint the scene? Does each location have its own signature sound?

### The Whisper Test
Can you whisper-read the last page? If it needs LOUD words — wrong ending.

### The "Again" Test
- One quotable line kids will repeat
- One memorable moment
- One feeling they recognize

### The Refrain Test
Is there a repeating line or pattern a 3-year-old can join in on by the third time?

### The Human Test
- Observed Moment present?
- Purposeless True Detail present?
- Slow Moment present?
- Performance Note present?

### The Parent Test
Would the parent feel something? Want to re-read? Smile at least once?

### The Discovery Test
Did anything surprise you while writing?

### The Heat Before Cooling Test
Fear held for a full page before comfort?

### The Sensory Tourism Test (for travel stories)
- Does each location have a unique sensory signature?
- Is culture shown through action, not explanation?
- Would a child who's never been there FEEL like they went?

---

## THE FINAL TEST

**Would this feel like it was written by someone who knows and loves THESE two specific boys?**

Not "children in general." Christopher who carries the weight. Benjamine who surprises everyone with his quiet honesty. If it could be about any two kids — rewrite.

**And: does it work in the dark?** No pictures. No screen glow needed. Just a parent's voice and two boys listening. If the words alone don't build the world — rewrite.
